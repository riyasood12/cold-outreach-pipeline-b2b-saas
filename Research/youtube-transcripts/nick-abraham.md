# Nick Abraham

The two most valuable videos for understanding modern B2B SaaS outbound systems from Nick Abraham are *Interview with Nick Abraham on Cold Email* (with Eric Nowoslawski) and *He Sends 1.5M Cold Emails Per MONTH* because together they cover both systems thinking and current execution around deliverability, infrastructure, messaging, signals, and scale.

---

## Video 1

**Title:** Interview with Nick Abraham on Cold Email

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iRxyDhQ6qDA

**Date:** November 15, 2024

**Note on speakers:** This is a conversation between **Nick Abraham** (Leadbird) and **Eric Nowoslawski** (Growth Engine X). Both are cold email agency owners at scale. Nick is the primary guest sharing infrastructure and campaign strategy; Eric is the co-host and peer contributor. The original .md attributed statements to "Eric" that were actually Nick's — attribution in this summary reflects the transcript accurately.

### Transcript Summary

- Cold email in late 2024 is harder than at any prior point, driven by two major spam filter updates that tanked deliverability across the industry. Both Nick and Eric are seeing the same patterns.

- **Infrastructure philosophy:** When deliverability drops, don't investigate — just assume the copy is generating too many spam complaints, set up new domains and inboxes immediately, and keep moving. The operational response to deliverability problems is speed of rotation, not root-cause analysis.

- **AB flip infrastructure model:** Run two complete sets of inboxes per client simultaneously. Use Set A for Month 1. Keep Set B warming the entire time. At the end of Month 1, flip to Set B (which has now had 30–45 days of warm-up). Put Set A back on warm-up. Rotate month on month. This passively heals domain reputation and gives you a ready backup if anything burns.

- **Backup domains at scale:** Nick's agency maintains 600+ domains in perpetual warm-up (bought during sales from Porkbun at discount, using .co and generic TLDs). If an infrastructure crisis hits, they can immediately release pre-warmed domains to any client. Domain age is increasingly important — buying generic aged domains in advance is infrastructure insurance.

- **What actually determines deliverability:** Bounce rate and spam complaint rate matter far more than send volume or technical configurations. As long as a campaign isn't being marked as spam, you can push volume aggressively. Nick tested sending 1,000 emails in a single day from one Gmail inbox to an opted-in list and still inboxed the next day. The lesson: it's the signal, not the number.

- **Email structure that works:** Every campaign is built around four elements:
  1. **Why you / why now line** (something noticed about their company that prompted the outreach — John Barrows' framing)
  2. **Pain agitation or offer alignment**
  3. **Outcome-based case study** ("We just helped [similar company] achieve X")
  4. **Light CTA** ("Would this be worth a conversation?" or "Is this something you'd be interested in?")

- **Demand capture vs. demand generation** (framework from Daniel Fosio / Client Ascension):
  - *Demand capture:* The buyer already knows they need your solution; you're finding the ones ready to buy now. Works best with high-volume broad sends. Risk: hard to stand out if the market is saturated with similar offers.
  - *Demand generation:* You're creating awareness of a problem or possibility the buyer didn't know existed. Works with smaller, more targeted lists. Produces higher engagement when the offer is genuinely novel.
  - The fix for a demand-capture offer that isn't converting: **narrow the list** so the message becomes more specific. A generic bookkeeping pitch to all founders is demand capture. The same pitch to first-time founders under 20 headcount whose company is less than two years old and who have no finance hire — that's a much tighter list where demand capture feels like demand generation.

- **"Below the signature" tactic:** Put a short, punchy message above the fold. Below the signature, add 4–6 case studies, a detailed process walkthrough, or social proof — so prospects who want more context get it without cluttering the main message. One contact Nick coached said this tripled their responses.

- **Pre-empting the main objection in the email:** Model: the fire department's recruiting banner that read "We're not recruiting heroes, we're recruiting neighbours." They knew the objection ("I'm not a hero") and pre-empted it directly. In cold email, identify the most likely reason a prospect will dismiss your offer and address it head-on in the copy.

- **Mechanism over case studies:** For an offshore staffing client, case studies weren't converting. Switching to a detailed description of their hiring process (90% of applicants cut in round 1, a paid task to test attention to detail, three punctuality-tested interviews, the hire works internally first) outperformed all case studies because it addressed the real objection — "I don't trust that you'll find me a good person." Mechanism builds trust when outcomes alone don't.

- **Personalisation placement matters more than personalisation volume:**
  - Putting a PS personalisation ("I wasn't sure if I should reach out to you or [colleague name]") performs very well across many offers.
  - Putting the same personalisation in the body opener sometimes kills the email.
  - Test placement — not just content — when personalisation isn't working.

- **"Cheesy intro lines" finding:** In saturated markets (e.g., e-commerce agencies), even well-written direct-response emails blend in because everyone is writing them. Counter-intuitively, a corny opener — *"I know your inbox is busier than a bee's nest"* — signals that a real human wrote this, which makes the prospect more open. Spin-tax the cheesy line so each send is unique.

- **Meme template:** Subject = "meme for you." Preview text = "Does this meme encapsulate [their pain point]?" Include a relevant meme in the body (e.g., the Suez Canal ship stuck = their team's bandwidth vs. the problem). The preview text drives opens; the meme creates a moment that gets people to reply.

### Key Insights

- Attribution: Nick Abraham is the primary content contributor in this video, not Eric. Key infrastructure strategies originate from Nick's agency (Leadbird).
- The AB flip model (two inbox sets, rotating monthly) is Nick's most-used infrastructure solution for sustained deliverability.
- Demand capture vs. demand generation is the primary lens for deciding campaign structure and list strategy — narrow the list when you can't change the offer.
- Mechanism descriptions outperform case studies when the real buyer objection is about trust in execution, not proof of outcomes.
- Personalisation placement (where in the email) matters as much as personalisation content.

## Video 2

**Title:** He Sends 1.5M Cold Emails Per MONTH, here's what works in 2025

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=POIU0L0Lsw8

**Date:** May 9, 2025

### Transcript Summary

- Nick runs 200+ active clients at Leadbird, sending well over 1.5M cold emails per month, with 90,000+ inboxes active in his SmartLead account. He was the second-most-replied-to account across all of SmartLead's 90,000+ users in the prior year.

- Cold email is viable in 2025 but harder, costlier, and less forgiving. The channel still produces the lowest cost of acquisition when the fundamentals are right.

- **The single most important variable is offer + product-market fit.** If you don't have those, nothing else matters — not cold email, not ads, not anything. Most people who say cold email is dead are actually running a hard offer in a demand-capture market without adapting the approach.

- **Demand capture vs. demand generation** (Nick's version):
  - Demand generation offers: people immediately recognise the value and want it. Run these with a broad list and high volume — you don't need signals or Clay workflows; just send.
  - Demand capture offers: you need to find the small % of the market actively looking. For these, **narrow the list and get creative with the angle** — turn a demand-capture offer into something that feels like demand generation by making a very specific claim to a very specific sub-segment.
  - Some demand-capture offers (e.g., "would you like to sell your business?") should just be run at volume — you're fishing for the small % who happen to be ready right now, and that's okay.

- **Infrastructure — 40/40/20 split:**
  - 40% of sending from Google Workspace inboxes
  - 40% from Microsoft Outlook inboxes
  - 20% from a trusted SMTP provider (Nick uses Mail Reef) as emergency backup
  - Rationale: Google and Microsoft IPs are trusted by the email ecosystem in a way third-party SMTPs are not. When Google/Microsoft accounts get nuked, SMTP gives you an 8% reply rate vs. 0% — and 8% beats nothing.

- **Monthly rotation model:** Buy two full sets of inboxes per client. Use Set 1 for Month 1. Keep Set 2 warming (30-day warm-up → becomes 45-day warm-up by the time you flip). Rotate. Passive domain reputation recovery during the off-month means you rarely need to buy new domains — just rotate.

- **Deliverability diagnostics — bounce analysis:**
  - All deliverability problems are visible in the bounce messages. Analyse them per domain.
  - If Google is rejecting emails from your domain with a "suspects spam" message, and this is happening in volume, that domain is cooked — retire it and move on.
  - Nick's team monitors the SmartLead global block list daily, extracts bounce data per domain, and flags any domain whose sender bounce rate exceeds 1–3%.

- **MX record filtering:** Identify prospects on your list who use enterprise email security tools (Barracuda, ProofPoint, Mimecast). Don't necessarily remove them — if you're targeting software companies, they all use these. Instead, **put them at the bottom of the send list** so your domain has more age and reputation by the time you reach them.

- **Lead list scoring:** Put your highest-confidence, best-fit accounts at the top of the send list. Getting early positive engagement from the best accounts builds domain reputation before you hit the harder-to-reach ones.

- **Deliverability basics that most people miss:**
  - Clean company names (write "Leadbird," not "Leadbird LLC")
  - Keep emails under 100 words
  - No open tracking
  - Send only to validated emails; run catch-alls in a separate campaign
  - Copy fatigue is real — when copy text has been in circulation too long, engagement drops even if the message is good

- **Reply rate benchmarks:**
  - Below 1% reply rate → deliverability issue, fix infrastructure first
  - 2–4% reply rate → solid; you're inboxing well and generating conversations
  - 20–40% positive reply rate → healthy depending on offer
  - **1 lead per 350–500 contacts** is Nick's target (a "lead" = someone who says "I'd love to set up a call")

- **CTA strategy:** Nick's default is a simple, conversational direct-response CTA: *"Is that something you'd be interested in?"* It gets people saying "yes, tell me more" or "yes, let's talk" and is low-friction enough to generate responses. However, in saturated markets, a lead magnet CTA (free mockup, free audit) may generate more replies even if they convert to meetings at a lower rate — model the full funnel to decide.

- **Signals — what actually works:**
  - Demand generation offers don't need signals — run volume and the offer does the work.
  - Demand capture offers benefit significantly from signals.
  - Nick's favourite current signal: **LinkedIn company page followers**. He originated the "scrape a competitor's LinkedIn followers and cold email them" playbook. Within 7 days of first using it for his own agency, they were signing clients left and right — shortest sales cycle they'd ever seen.
  - New signal he's watching: **monitoring a competitor's sales reps' LinkedIn connections**. If a competitor's AE connects with someone, that person is likely in an active sales process with the competitor — which makes them a warm signal for you.
  - **Critical signal routing principle:** high-intent signals should be routed to a human SDR or AE for manual engagement — not auto-dumped into a SmartLead or Instantly campaign. Signals have more intent; they deserve human follow-up, not automated sequences. Nick saw zero results when routing signals to cold email campaigns; started seeing deals when an AE worked them manually.

- **Clay use cases (practical, not abstract):**
  - **Colleague injection:** Find a colleague in the same department and inject their name — *"I wasn't sure if I should reach out to you or [colleague name]."* Works on any offer.
  - **ICP identification:** Use Clay to identify what a prospect's company sells and to whom. Then reference it in the email — *"I noticed you sell [product] to [customer type]."*
  - **Competitor mention:** Find 2–3 competitors for each prospect and reference them in the email to create relevance.
  - Avoid using Clay to generate entire emails — AI-written full emails are detectable and feel inhuman. Use Clay for a single variable or filter, not full copy.

- **Stay simple until proven:** Don't build complex Clay workflows before you've validated that the offer, list, and basic copy work. Fancy automations on top of a broken offer don't fix the offer — they just automate failure faster.

### Key Insights

- The 40/40/20 infrastructure split (Google / Microsoft / SMTP) is Nick's named, practical model for sustainable sending at scale.
- Monthly rotation of inbox sets is the most cost-effective deliverability maintenance technique — passive healing during the off-month.
- Bounce message analysis per domain is the correct diagnostic tool for deliverability problems — not GlockApps tests or blacklist checks.
- High-intent signals must be routed to human SDRs, not automated campaigns — that is the most common and costly signal misuse Nick sees.
- Reply rate benchmarks (1% floor, 2–4% target, 1 lead per 350–500 contacts) are practical thresholds for evaluating campaign health.

## Why These Two?

Video 1 covers campaign-level strategy: offer framing, email structure, the demand-capture vs. demand-generation lens, personalisation tactics, mechanism vs. case study trade-offs, and creative formats (meme template, below-the-signature).
