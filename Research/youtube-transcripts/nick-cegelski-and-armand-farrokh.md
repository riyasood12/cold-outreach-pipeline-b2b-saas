### Transcript Summary

- Armand Farrokh and Nick Cegelski argue that cold call openers must break the telemarketer stigma immediately by leading with context — something specific to the prospect — rather than generic permission lines or pleasantries.

- **Two named opener styles:**

  **1. Heard the Name Tossed Around opener (Armand's):**
  Lead with how you work with people like them (mutual investor, peers in the same office, same title in the same city), introduce yourself, then ask if they've heard your name tossed around.
  - *"Hey Nick, we work with a few other partners in the Skadden LA office. It's Armand from Northwestern. Have you heard my name tossed around?"*
  - It doesn't matter if they have. What matters is sounding like they should have. Tone: feet up on the desk, like you're calling a referral, not a cold prospect.
  - If they say no: *"Oh, I guess I'm not as popular as I thought. Well, the reason for my call is…"* then drop into the problem proposition.

  **2. Tailored Permission opener (Nick's):**
  Lead with a piece of context you can directly connect to what you sell, own the fact that it's a cold call, and ask for 30 seconds.
  - *"Emma, I just finished reading your press release about the new office opening. I'm going to be honest — this is a cold call, but it's a well-researched one. I'm wondering if I can get 30 seconds to share why that press release prompted me to call, and you can totally hang up if it doesn't make sense."*
  - The context must connect to the pitch. If you cite a press release about a new office, the pitch should relate to something a new office creates (costs, billing complexity, headcount growth, etc.).

- **Pitch framework — Problem Proposition:**
  1. A triggering problem described with vivid specificity (see "hairy lollipop" below)
  2. One sentence on the solution, framed as: "We do X to solve that problem"
  3. Low-friction, interest-based CTA: *"Are you open to taking a look?"* — not "can we book 30 minutes?" You're selling interest, not a meeting.

- **Gong data on problem vs. product language:** Problem-based language → **16% meeting success rate**. Product buzzwords (single source of truth, all-in-one platform, leading provider) → **5.5% success rate**. Three times more effective.

- **Mr. Miyagi objection method (three steps):**
  1. **Agree** — remove all pressure. *"Totally get it. Nowadays it's harder to keep a tool, let alone buy something new."*
  2. **Incentivize conversation** — get them talking by offering an exit. *"Just so no one from my team cold calls you again, could you give me a sense — is budget completely out for this cycle, or is it that anything you try to buy has to go through the full ringer?"*
  3. **Sell the test drive, not the car** — sell the meeting as a low-stakes preview, not a buying decision. *"Look, you're probably not going to buy this right now, but every once in a while, the people who get budget when it's released are the ones who know what's one, two, and three on their wishlist. Would you be open to taking a look — not for now, but just so you know it's out there?"*
  - Key: prospects aren't objecting to your product. They're objecting to the cold call interruption. Don't handle the objection — handle the reaction.

- Live call examples throughout the video reinforce that tone, delivery, and confidence carry the message as much as the exact words.

### Key Insights

- The only opener that cannot be canned is one that leads with something specific to the prospect — context is the differentiator.
- Problem-led pitching is three times more effective than product-led pitching per Gong data.
- The Mr. Miyagi method works because it removes the pressure of the pitch before trying to redirect — agreeing with the objection is the disarm, not a concession.

## Video 2

**Title:** Cold Calling Masterclass: 52 Minutes of Proven Cold Calling Tips (For B2B Sales)

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gtKZ7fP1HZM

**Date:** July 18, 2024

### Transcript Summary

#### Is Cold Calling Worth It?

- Data from **300 million cold calls** analysed with Gong:
  - Average rep (50th percentile): 5.5% connect rate on 800 dials = 43 conversations → 4.6% set rate = **2 meetings per month**
  - Top quartile rep: 13.3% connect rate = 106 conversations → 16.7% set rate = **18 meetings per month**
  - Nine times more meetings from the same 800 dials — not from dialing more, but from being better.

- Voicemail multiplier: leaving a voicemail **more than doubles cold email reply rate** — from 1.81% to 3.44%. So even if nobody calls back, voicemail is worth leaving.

- The bar is low. Most cold callers use canned openers, product buzzwords, and generic pitches. Being marginally better puts you in the top quartile.

#### The First 60 Seconds — Two Openers

**1. Heard the Name Tossed Around (Armand):**
- Lead with how you work with their peers, introduce yourself, ask if they've heard your name tossed around.
- Tone: call a referral, not a stranger. Assume familiarity.
- If they say no: *"I guess I'm not as popular as I thought — well, the reason for my call is…"*

**2. Tailored Permission Opener (Nick):**
- Lead with a specific piece of context tied directly to what you sell.
- Own the cold call honestly: *"I'm going to be honest, this is a cold call, but it's a well-researched one."*
- Ask for 30 seconds, offer to hang up if it doesn't make sense.
- If they say they can't give permission: *"Shoot, totally my bad. Just so nobody from my team bugs you again — is it that I caught you at a horrible time, or is it that you already know what we do and this just isn't interesting at all?"*

#### Problem Proposition (not Value Proposition)

- Three problems with traditional value propositions:
  1. Vague buzzwords that don't mean anything to the prospect
  2. Solutions have no context without a problem
  3. People act on pain, not benefits (you take Advil without fail; you skip multivitamins)

- **Hairy lollipop specificity:** Don't say "people hate running merit cycles." Say: *"Imagine you're an attorney who just won a big case. You submit your billing to the insurance carrier. They reject it because one line item doesn't match their guidelines — and now you're three weeks into back-and-forth appeals just to get paid for work you already did."* Scenery + specific tasks + specific annoyances = triggered prospect.

- **Problem proposition structure:**
  1. Triggering problem (vivid, specific, makes them sit up)
  2. One-sentence solution: *"We work with 300 law firms helping them cut appeals by alerting attorneys to guideline violations in real time."*
  3. Interest-based CTA: *"I know I got you out of the blue — are you open to taking a look when I'm not calling you cold?"*

#### Objection Handling — Mr. Miyagi Method (Full Detail)

- **Agree:** Pull out the chair. *"I totally get it. Honestly, I should have assumed you'd be all set — otherwise you probably would have reached out to us."* Surprise them by not pushing back.

- **Incentivize conversation:** Use multiple choice to make it easy for them to share. *"Just so no one cold calls you again — is it because budget for this cycle is completely out, or is it that anything you try to buy goes through the whole ringer?"* Multiple choice reduces cognitive load and gets them talking.

- **Sell the test drive:** Once they've shared a little, use what they said to frame a no-pressure meeting. *"You're probably not going to buy this thing — but in my experience, when budget does free up, the people who get it are the ones who already know what's on their wishlist. Would you be open to taking a look, just so you know it's out there?"*

- Practice tip: on your commute, repeat the agree phrase and the test drive phrase until they are automatic. When you have to think about what to say during an objection, you lose the moment.

#### Gatekeepers — Triple Bypass

Three attempts in sequence, dosing information bit by bit:

1. **Slide by:** *"Hey, could you get me through to Aubrey? It's Nick."* Minimal information. Ask where you want to go.

2. **Lean on context:** If they push back — *"Yeah, I work with a few other partners in the LA office. Would you mind letting him know it's Nick Cegelski?"* End with a question; the person asking questions controls the conversation.

3. **Lean on social proof:** If they need more — *"Yeah, we help a couple of other partners in the office with their tax planning. It's Northwestern Mutual. I actually sent him a note the other day — would you mind giving him a heads up? It's Nick."*

- Never pitch the gatekeeper — they're trained to detect pitches, not evaluate them.
- Never befriend them insincerely — they know what you're doing.
- Never lie — you'll see them again when scheduling the meeting.

#### Voicemail — Double Tap Rule

Leave only two voicemails. Direct to email, not callback.

**Voicemail 1 (15 seconds) — context only, no pitch:**
*"Hey Nick, we work with a few Skadden partners in the LA office. No need to call me back — I'm literally about to hit send on an email. Just so we don't play phone tag, would you mind replying and letting me know if it's even moderately interesting? It'll come from Armand at Northwestern. Cheers."*

**Voicemail 2 (30 seconds) — context + social proof:**
*"Hey Nick, we work with a few other Skadden partners in the LA office on their tax planning, among other things. I'm sure you've got that handled, but I'm about to hit send on an email — just to give you a sense of how we're helping them, and so we don't play phone tag, would you mind replying and letting me know if it's even moderately interesting? It'll come from Armand at Northwestern. Cheers."*

- If two voicemails don't work, stop leaving them — they're flagging your number.
- Rotate your outbound number to avoid spam-likely tags.

#### Off-Phone Operating System

**Golden three conversion metrics:**
1. **Connect rate** — what % of dials become live conversations?
2. **Set rate** — what % of live conversations become meetings?
3. **Show rate** — what % of booked meetings actually show up?

**Boosting connect rate (four rules):**
1. Call mobile and direct lines — higher pickup, bypasses gatekeeper
2. Mark your tracks — note red (wrong number), yellow (uncertain), green (confirmed right person) on every number so you never waste a dial twice
3. Law of diminishing returns — 5 dials over 4 weeks with no answer? Move on. Impassable gatekeeper? Move on. Two voicemails left? Stop leaving more.
4. Avoid spam-likely tagging — don't repeatedly call bad numbers; don't spike your dial volume; rotate numbers; test new numbers by calling a friend before using them on prospects

**Boosting set rate — A/B/C tier prospect prioritisation:**
- **A tier:** Buying now — prospect has an active trigger (upcoming compensation cycle, hiring for a relevant role, just closed funding, just opened a new office)
- **B tier:** Has the problem, not necessarily right now — growing headcount, recent org change, etc.
- **C tier:** Anyone else in your total addressable market
- Call A and B tier prospects twice as often. Build your list in reverse — start with 200, cut disqualified, segment into C (50%), B (25%), A (25%).

- Intent signals that should trigger an immediate call: prospect opened your email 4+ times; replied with any objection; visited your pricing page; just announced a funding round.

**Boosting show rate:**
- Confirm the calendar invite **on the call**: *"I just sent you an invite for 2pm tomorrow. Would you mind hitting accept right now? I'll give you a couple seconds."* In enterprise, invites often don't hit calendars without explicit acceptance.
- Send a confirmation email the day before (or one week before + day before for meetings set far out) that includes one piece of research — showing you prepared makes cancellation feel costly.
- If they no-show: at 2 minutes send an email with the Zoom link; at 4 minutes call; at 6 minutes call again. Never delete the invite — move it 3–4 days forward and offer backup times.

**Hairy lollipop specificity (reminder):** Include scenery, specific tasks, specific annoyances in problem descriptions. Dangerously specific beats vaguely general — even if you overshoot their exact problem, the specificity signals that you understand people like them.

**Maximising dial volume — 40-calls-in-an-hour framework:**
1. Go into the dial blitz with **60 call tasks pre-loaded** — research done, contacts found, list prepped. Not bouncing between research and calling.
2. Set a **minimum 60-minute blitz** — the first 5–6 dials always have rust; amortise that startup cost over a full hour, not 30 minutes.
3. Set a **40-dial goal** — a number target, not just a time target, eliminates internal negotiation about when to stop.
4. **Close everything else** — no inbox, no Slack, no Instagram during the blitz.
5. **No lingering** — send the calendar invite after a meeting set and immediately dial the next number. Don't wallow in a hung-up call. The phone should always be ringing or connected.

**Time-blocking system — green/yellow/red hours:**
- **Green hours (8am–12pm):** Prospecting only. Two blocks: cold calling first, then emailing. Hit your prospecting goal before noon.
- **Yellow hours (12pm–3pm):** Customer meetings. Schedule live calls here — you've got energy and you've already hit your prospecting target.
- **Red hours (3pm–6pm):** Everything else — one-on-ones, account research, list building, admin. These don't require daylight business hours, so push them to the end of the day.
- Time block your inbox to three windows per day (8:30am, 12pm, 3pm). Close it completely outside those windows. Do the same with internal Slack.

### Key Insights

- Cold calling is worth it at scale — but only if you're in the top quartile. The 9x meeting gap between average and top performers is driven by connect rate discipline and set rate skill, not dial volume.
- Context is the only non-canned opener ingredient — everything else can be templated and detected.
- Problem-based language is 3x more effective than product buzzwords on cold calls (Gong data).
- The Mr. Miyagi method works because it removes pressure before redirecting — the agree step is the disarm, not a concession.
- The off-phone system (green/yellow/red hours, 40-call framework, A/B/C tiers, golden three metrics) produces more meetings from the same number of dials by eliminating inefficiency, not by adding effort.
