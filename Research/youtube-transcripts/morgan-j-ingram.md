# Morgan J. Ingram

The two most valuable videos for understanding modern B2B SaaS outbound systems from Morgan J. Ingram are *Building next-level pipeline* and *Outbound Prospecting on LinkedIn* because together they cover Morgan's modern view of multichannel prospecting, LinkedIn-led outreach, cold call structure, messaging quality, AI use, and the shift from generic outbound to buyer-relevant pipeline systems.

---

## Video 1

**Title:** Building next-level pipeline | Morgan J. Ingram (Amp Creative)

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5NfqaBU63AI

**Date:** June 30, 2025

### Transcript Summary

- The number one mistake teams make is talking about features and functions instead of speaking the language of the buyer's industry. Morgan uses an analogy: British English is still English, but if someone says "this is a bit dodgy," an American doesn't know what they mean. Most reps are doing the equivalent — technically speaking, but not in the buyer's dialect. The fix: immerse yourself in the buyer's world. Listen to their podcasts, read their newsletters and white papers, until you sound like you live in their industry.

- **Read your emails out loud test:** Morgan ran this exercise with a team whose messaging wasn't working. He asked them to read their own emails aloud, then asked "Would anyone respond to this?" Everyone said no. That revealed the real problem. He also recommends sending email drafts to an internal person who matches the buyer profile — if the internal CMO wouldn't respond, the external one won't either.

- **Don't scale before mastering fundamentals.** Scaling bad prospecting just creates more bad prospecting. First validate: do you understand how someone buys? Can you do discovery? Do you understand the buyer? Then figure out what to scale.

- **Cold call structure (universal, every time):**
  1. **Intro / hook** (5–8 seconds) — the hardest part; if you lose them here, nothing else matters
  2. **Value prop** (what you do, in context) — if you monologue for more than 30 seconds, there is an **80% drop-off rate**; industry jargon or confusing language kills this stage
  3. **Objections** (3–4 max) — don't let them throw more than 4 objections; past that, they're interrogating you to get all their questions answered for free and will hang up
  4. **Close** (book the meeting)

- **PLA framework for the intro (Morgan's specific named technique):**
  - **P — Pleasant:** Open with *"Hi Becca, thanks for taking my call."* Most people aren't thanked for picking up. This immediately disrupts the pattern and puts the prospect on the same psychological level as you.
  - **L — Laugh:** A small natural chuckle creates levity and lowers the prospect's guard.
  - **A — Arms Up:** Physically raising your arms changes your vocal inflection — the voice becomes more curious and less aggressive, making the prospect more open to continuing.
  - Full opener: *"Hi Becca, thanks for taking my call. Hey look, I know I'm calling out of the blue here. However, do you have a few moments to chat?"*
  - Note: Morgan doesn't introduce himself by name first — "I'm not that important unless I'm The Rock."

- **"I'm not interested" comeback script:** When a prospect tries to end the call immediately, don't hang up or capitulate.
  - *"Hey, completely understand that you probably don't like cold calls. Look, I sometimes don't like making them. But all I'm asking for is 30 seconds of your time. In that 30 seconds, I'll explain what we do here at [company]. If it makes sense, we could put time on the calendar. If not, you can hang up on me. Does that sound fair?"*
  - Key mechanics: give them a self-created exit (the hang-up option), so they don't need to invent one; end with "Does that sound fair?" because no one wants to appear unfair.
  - In Morgan's experience across training 50,000+ people: no one has ever physically come after him for asking one more question. People forget about the call in seconds — fear of follow-up is irrational.

- **Multichannel is not optional.** Many buyers have their phones on Do Not Disturb or have unknown numbers silenced. Phone alone won't reach everyone. The answer is using multiple channels — phone, email, LinkedIn, events — because you don't know where a given prospect "lives." Some respond only on LinkedIn; some only pick up the phone.

- **LinkedIn profile headline as SEO:** Your LinkedIn headline is searchable on Google. No buyer wakes up and searches for "account executives." They search for solutions to their problems. Rewrite your headline around the buyer's pain points, not your job title. One rep changed his headline from "AE at [company]" to buyer pain points and saw an **80% increase in connection acceptance rate** and a **300% increase in profile views**.

- **Blank connection requests + video + voice notes:** Send blank (no message) connection requests — the headline does the work. Once connected, stand out with video and voice notes. AI bots are flooding LinkedIn with text messages. Buyers are increasingly unsure which messages are human. A video or voice note signals a real person immediately.

- **Data point from a sales leader:** He polled his team of 30 STRs' executives — CFOs, CTOs, CISOs, CMOs, CEOs. **All of them said they would prefer a LinkedIn video over any other outreach format. All of them had received zero LinkedIn videos in the past 12 months.** The opportunity is wide open.

- **AI — the right and wrong way:**
  - Wrong: tell AI to "write a great email." It doesn't know what a great email is and will produce a bad one.
  - Right: ask your executives for the top 5–10 messages they have actually responded to. Collect 50 examples. Feed them to AI and ask: "What made these good? What's the common thread?" AI extracts the framework. Now you have a proven model to generate from.
  - AI works for: call summaries, identifying objection patterns, running simulations to practice handling recurring objections.
  - AI doesn't work for: AI comments on LinkedIn (the route is wrong — even if the visibility logic is sound, spammy AI comments damage your reputation over time).

### Key Insights

- Modern outbound works when reps sound like industry insiders, not product narrators — immersion in the buyer's world is the skill, not memorising features.
- The PLA framework (Pleasant, Laughing, Arms Up) is Morgan's specific, named intro technique — not just "be friendly" but a structured physical and verbal approach to the first 5 seconds.
- The 80% drop-off stat for value props over 30 seconds is the clearest argument for brevity in the pitch section.
- LinkedIn is a core outbound channel — not a brand play — when the profile is optimised for buyer search and outreach is done with native human formats (video, voice).
- AI amplifies good inputs; it multiplies bad ones at scale.

## Video 2

**Title:** Outbound Prospecting on LinkedIn | Leadfeeder's B2B Rebellion

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tdg6YBZHF8o

**Date:** September 28, 2020

### Transcript Summary

- Traditional cold email and cold call response rates are declining, which makes LinkedIn a primary outbound channel — not a side channel. Morgan references a client who shifted from 30% of outbound meetings coming from LinkedIn to **70% of all outbound meetings coming from LinkedIn** (InMails, videos, voice messages, commenting, connection requests).

- LinkedIn works because buyers are more engaged there than anywhere else. They're not commuting or travelling; they're spending more time on social. LinkedIn native formats — video, voice, InMail — cut through in a way that email increasingly does not.

- **10-30-10 Framework (Morgan's named LinkedIn video messaging structure):**

  - **First 10 seconds — Trigger or Persona Priority:**
    Choose one of two angles:
    - *Trigger:* Something you found on their profile or website — a new hire, a promotion, a change in leadership, a job posting. Say: *"The reason for my video is…"* (stating this gives you confidence and signals you're not winging it).
    - *Priority:* Something you know matters to their role — e.g., "As an enablement leader, one of the top priorities I know you have right now is helping reps communicate with real business acumen." Pick one trigger or one priority, not both.

  - **Middle 30 seconds — Value Proposition:**
    Use the same value prop you'd use on a cold call. Don't reinvent the message; transfer it into the video.

  - **Last 10 seconds — Conversational CTA:**
    Avoid hard asks. High-performing CTAs: *"Are you open to learning more?"* or *"Would you be interested in a deeper dialogue?"* — conversational, low-pressure, easy to say yes to.

- **The 10-30-10 applies at any point in the sequence** — not just first touch. Whether you're opening outreach or re-engaging someone who's gone quiet, the structure holds. The trigger or priority angle simply becomes more specific the further into a relationship you are.

- **LinkedIn video is underused.** At the time of the video, almost no SDRs were sending LinkedIn videos. Morgan's point: there's still a wide open opportunity for reps who overcome the fear. The fear of being on camera is less costly than the risk of being ignored.

### Key Insights

- The 10-30-10 framework is Morgan's most actionable and transferable LinkedIn outreach tool — simple enough to learn in one session, flexible enough to use at any sequence stage.
- 70% of outbound meetings from LinkedIn (for the case study client) is the data point that establishes LinkedIn as a primary channel, not a supplement.
- Trigger vs. priority is a meaningful distinction in the first 10 seconds — pick one and commit; don't try to combine them.
- Human formats (video, voice) cut through AI-generated noise on LinkedIn in a way that text messages increasingly cannot.
