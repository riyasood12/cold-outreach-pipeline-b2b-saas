# Jason Bay

The two most valuable Jason Bay videos for understanding modern B2B SaaS outbound systems are his short messaging masterclass and his long-form cold-calling framework interview, because together they cover message design, offer creation, call structure, coaching, metrics, and multichannel execution in the current outbound environment.

---

## Video 1

**Title:** How to Build a Perfect Outbound Sales Pitch with Jason Bay

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IywS6oZwKq4

**Date:** October 25, 2024

### Transcript Summary

- Jason says outbound messaging fails when it is written by siloed teams — marketing or enablement — who do not regularly speak to customers and have never used the messaging themselves.

- He recommends building a cross-functional "messaging team" of 8–10 people: top reps, sales leaders, marketing, and subject-matter experts (e.g., ex-practitioners from the buyer's world). This team creates messaging; sales then makes it sequence-ready.

- Good messaging is built around four components in sequence: **priorities → status quo (how they solve it today) → problems → aspirations**. Priorities get the most focus.

- He uses contact-center leaders as the worked example: the customer-voice line is "we hear that contact center leaders want to reduce cost to serve, but can't figure out why customers are calling in so they can prevent it" — not "we have a comprehensive dashboard to manage your contact center."

- Gong data: **80%+ of buyers believe the sales reps they speak with don't understand what they do on a day-to-day basis** — the core reason customer-voice messaging outperforms product language.

- Segmentation upfront reduces customisation effort later. A contact-center leader at Nordstrom (retail, B2C returns) is a completely different persona from one at Mercedes (B2B dealerships). Getting the message 80–90% there by segment means reps spend their energy on individual tailoring, not figuring out what to say.

- Four ways to upgrade messaging quickly:
  1. Bring in expert consultants who are former buyers (e.g., an ex-CISO at Coca-Cola for a cybersecurity client) to stress-test the language.
  2. Workshop with your best reps only — not the whole org.
  3. Run 30-minute customer fireside chats that the sales team attends and pulls quotes from.
  4. Extract patterns from recorded calls — the most underrated method. Go back to existing call recordings and pull the language customers use about their own problems.

- The offer is as important as the message. Rain Group stat: **58% of meetings buyers attend feel like a complete waste of time**. Prospects are desensitised to "I'd love 15 minutes." The fix is promising something valuable in return.

- Two worked offer examples:
  - **Free website audit** (e-commerce client): "I'm going to show you two or three things making you lose sales to competitors, or I'll show you things that will increase checkout conversion by 20–30%."
  - **Mystery-shop report** (outsourced chat client): The company mystery-shopped 200 e-commerce businesses and used findings to open cold calls with a specific observation — e.g., "I was on your website and was alarmed that it took 24 hours to get a response from your chat team. By the way, it's Jason with [company]. You got a minute for me to share the reason for my call?" — leading with a researched problem, not a pitch.

### Key Insights

- Strong outbound systems begin with message architecture, not sequence volume.
- The four-component framework (priorities → status quo → problems → aspirations) gives reps a repeatable structure for building customer-voice messaging.
- Gong's 80%+ stat is the clearest data-backed argument for why product-led messaging fails and customer-voice messaging wins.
- "Give-to-get" offers — audits, benchmark reports, mystery-shop findings — make cold outreach credible and materially improve meeting quality by de-risking the prospect's time investment.
- Customer-language extraction from real recorded calls is the most underrated and highest-fidelity input for better outbound messaging.

## Video 2

**Title:** Cold Calling Isn't Dead, BUT You Need To Be Top 20% To Make It Work

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4TuHuaZe0bU

**Date:** January 9, 2026

### Transcript Summary

- Cold calling is harder than it was two years ago, but still works for top teams. Half of Rippling's pipeline comes from outbound cold calling.

- **Benchmark data (Gong + 30MPC):**
  - Average connect rate: ~5.4%; top performers: 13–15%+
  - Average set rate (connected call → qualified meeting): ~4.6%; top performers: 16–18%+, with some teams reaching 22–35%
  - Average show rate from cold-set meetings: ~56%; world-class: 75–80%

- The biggest outbound upgrade right now is **the offer**. A demo of your product is not enough. Examples: a site audit that shows load-time issues and checkout friction before the call; a mystery-shop of hundreds of companies in a category used to open with a specific finding.

- **Permission-based opener (PBO):** The average cold call lasts 80 seconds. People pick up cold calls by accident — they think you're someone else. The PBO buys time and earns attention in those first seconds.
  - Gong data: using a PBO gives **2–4x the success rate** vs "did I catch you at a bad time?" or "how's it going?"
  - Nukes data nuance: 53% of top reps don't use a formal PBO but do use context; 41% use a PBO with context. **94% of top reps use context in some form.** Context is the real variable, not the exact opener format.
  - Example PBO with research baked in: *"Hey Eric, I was just on your website and tried to add something to the cart and it didn't work. By the way, it's Jason with XYZ company. You got a minute for me to share the reason for my call?"*

- **Reverse pitch:** After the opener, don't describe the product. Describe the problems that people like them are having, in customer language.
  - Gong data: problem-based language → **16% meeting success rate** vs. 5.5% for product buzzwords.
  - Example: *"We're working with several business owners in the Seattle area, and the number one thing we hear is they want to add an e-com component but it's so complicated with the tooling and limited staff — they can't open up sales outside their local region. I'm curious, is that something you're thinking about right now?"*

- **Brand vs. no-brand dynamics:** When calling from a well-known brand, prospects may already use your product. Anticipate this objection and build it into the opener: *"Hey, it's Jason from XYZ. I know you're already set up with our video solution — a lot of our customers are having challenges with X and Y. Do you have a minute?"* When calling from an unknown brand, nailing the problem language matters even more — if you speak in customer voice, people don't care what the company is called.

- **Money questions / question stacking:** World-class reps don't do full discovery on a cold call, but they do ask 1–2 "money questions" to expose a gap. Stack context before the question — don't ask "what's your biggest challenge?" Instead: *"I'm curious about what you mentioned with the FAQ pages — what we often see is that customers aren't extracting insights from chats and emails to identify why people call in. How are you collecting information like that to prevent inbound calls?"*

- **Show rate tactics:**
  - Confirm the calendar invite on the call: *"I just sent you an invite for 2pm tomorrow — would you mind hitting accept right now? I'll give you a couple seconds."* (Enterprise prospects often won't see an invite unless they accept it from within their email.)
  - Restate the purpose of the meeting in buyer language, not product language: *"We'll talk more about reducing cost to serve and how Nordstrom tackled a similar problem — if it makes sense we'll discuss fit."*
  - Humanise the AE: *"You'll be speaking with [name], who has 7–8 years working with retail contact centres. You're basically setting up a blind date — hype them up so the prospect doesn't want to bail on a real person."*

- **Three non-negotiable principles:**
  1. **Habit over outcome** — making calls regularly matters more than how any one block went.
  2. **Customer voice over product voice** — when in doubt, speak from the buyer's world about their problems.
  3. **Stay human and conversational** — don't transform into a different person on a call; casual and professional is not a contradiction.

- **AI for outbound prep — Prompt → Dig → Summarise framework:**
  - Prompt: tell the AI what you sell, who you sell to, and what specific signals to look for in 10-Ks, 10-Qs, earnings calls, and press releases.
  - Dig: the AI won't always get it right first pass — push it to go deeper on relevant findings and discard irrelevant ones.
  - Summarise: have it output a usable account plan with talking points for the cold call.
  - ChatGPT voice mode is specifically recommended for cold-call role play — better than dedicated role-play tools; can simulate the CMO at your target account, ramp up difficulty, and handle specific objections.

- **Weekly outbound rhythm for leaders (AE self-sourcing):**
  - Insert outbound into existing meetings — don't create new ones.
  - One-on-ones: reps bring their target accounts for the week plus draft messaging.
  - Team meetings: pull call recordings, report on activity, create a scoreboard.
  - Mid-week check-in: on Wednesday, check in with any rep not at 50% of their activity target — don't wait until Monday.

- **Rep-level coaching diagnostic — diagnose by layer:**
  1. Activity (are they making enough dials?)
  2. Connect rate (are they calling the right numbers at the right times? Using cell phone data? Not showing as spam?)
  3. Talk time per call (short average talk time = intro/opener problem)
  4. Conversion rate on connected calls
  5. Show rate (below 75% = confirmation process problem)

### Key Insights

- Modern outbound is an operational system as much as a messaging system — outcomes depend on data quality, timing, offer design, coaching, and measurable inspection at every stage.
- The best reps win by combining context, problem-based language, and customer voice — not brand recognition or scripts alone.
- Offer quality is now the primary lever: the question is not "can I get 15 minutes?" but "what will I give the buyer in return for their time?"
- The PBO + reverse pitch combination is Jason's core call structure, supported by Gong data at every step.
- The coaching framework links frontline tactics to manager-led inspection through five sequential diagnostic layers.

## Why These Two?

Video 1 explains how to design outbound messaging and offers from the ground up. Video 2 shows how to execute those messages on a cold call and how leaders should coach and inspect the motion. Together they give a complete picture of Jason Bay's outbound system: segment tightly, speak in customer language, make a compelling offer, open with a permission-based + relevance hook, reverse-pitch the problem, ask money questions, confirm the meeting on the call, and coach the motion through measurable stages.
