# Eric Nowoslawski

## Post 1
Date: ~June 16, 2026 

URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundphd_there-is-no-such-thing-as-a-b2b-saas-filter-activity-7462581598727708673-r1xf

Summary: Industry filters like "B2B SaaS" or "financial services" produce junk lists because companies self-categorize by identity, not buying behavior. Nowoslawski shares examples (e.g., Bacardi filed under beverage manufacturing) and describes defining verticals in writing, then qualifying companies in with custom rules.

Key Takeaway: Don't filter your way to a vertical—define what the segment means for your offer, pull broad, then qualify in with explicit inclusion/exclusion logic.

Category: Prospecting

## Post 2

Date: ~May 18, 2026

URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundphd_heres-something-nobody-talks-about-in-cold-activity-7440385354169204736-4w5f

Summary: Across 50+ Growth Engine X clients on identical infrastructure, deliverability diverges mainly by offer quality—not technical setup. Low reply rates trigger a spam-assumption cycle that burns inboxes; strong offers create a virtuous engagement loop that extends inbox lifespan.

Key Takeaway: Before tuning infrastructure, check reply rate—under ~1% often signals an offer problem, not a deliverability problem.

Category: Deliverability

## Post 3
Date: ~May 18, 2026 

URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundphd_we-automatically-pulled-35-sales-signals-activity-7457832794098978816-4KG_

Summary: For a client needing growth signals from investor filings (AI investment, capex changes, market outlook, etc.), the team used Parallel Extract, Codex sub-agents, and GPT-5 nano batch API instead of Claygent—which stops after finding one signal.

Key Takeaway: When you need many signals per account, use extraction orchestration that pulls everything in one pass rather than goal-oriented agents that stop at the first hit.

## Post 4
Date: ~April 18, 2026 

URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundphd_i-think-most-people-are-wasting-their-linkedin-activity-7424093019735412736-4B8m

Summary: LinkedIn shouldn't mirror email sequencing—use limited daily connection requests on high-intent targets: ICP engaging with case-study customers (name-drop in social proof), website de-anonymization visitors, and ICP engaging with competitor or topic posts.

Key Takeaway: Treat LinkedIn as a scarce, high-reply channel for your hottest leads-not a follow-up touch after email.

## Post 5
Date: ~February 18, 2026

URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundphd_this-is-the-type-of-campaign-i-would-suggest-activity-7419050979062358016-mgZ2

Summary: Nowoslawski recommends the "creative ideas" campaign for teams new to automated outbound: AI analyzes each prospect's website and outputs 1–3 specific ways the product could help—replacing generic AI compliments with tangible, context-based ideas.

Key Takeaway: Lead with concrete, website-derived ideas for how you could help—not feature lists or hollow personalization.

Category: Cold Email

## Cross-Cutting Themes

List quality beats filter convenience — Broad pulls plus written ICP definitions and qualification outperform naive industry/SaaS filters.

Engagement drives deliverability — Reply rates and offer strength matter as much as warmup, volume caps, and domain setup.

Match channel to intent and scale — Email scales; LinkedIn is for high-intent, limited-volume plays with real context.
