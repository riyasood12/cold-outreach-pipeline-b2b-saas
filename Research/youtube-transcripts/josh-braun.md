# Josh Braun

The two most valuable Josh Braun videos for understanding modern B2B SaaS outbound are his outbound philosophy interview and his cold-calling opener breakdown, because together they cover why outbound now requires detachment, disqualification, illuminating questions, and human conversation rather than scripted selling.

---

## Video 1

**Title:** Josh Braun on Outbound: Why It's Not Your Job to Sell to Everyone

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uT6uIAAgFGU

**Date:** February 4, 2026

### Transcript Summary

- Josh's core philosophy is **detachment from outcome** — not apathy, but a deliberate decision to release attachment to getting a specific result from every call. The origin of this mindset: as a teacher preparing for a dream job interview, he did everything right, was certain he'd get it, and didn't. He realised his job was to prepare and perform his best — not to control the outcome. That lesson transferred directly to sales.

- Detachment is a practised skill, not a personality trait. Josh links it to mindfulness: noticing your thoughts before reacting, so you don't turn into a different, more pushy version of yourself the moment someone objects.

- The mantra he has used for 15 years and teaches at workshops: *"It's not my job to sell everyone. My job is to illuminate potential problems, see if that person thinks it's a problem, and not steer the ship."*

- Modern outbound fails not because of bad scripts but because of wrong intent. If your intent is to book a meeting with everyone, you say things that feel pushy. Prospects can sense the commission breath immediately and pull away, because **autonomy is a basic human need**. The correct intent: go into every call asking "is there even a problem here?" — not assuming there is one.

- **Illuminating questions over pitching:** Instead of explaining and convincing (which creates resistance), ask a neutral question that illuminates a potential problem and passes the baton. The prospect's answer tells you whether to continue or move on.
  - Worked example — the Fit to Run store: A salesperson saw Josh's running shoes, asked "Are you a runner?" → "Got any races coming up?" → "You've probably had a running gait test." Josh hadn't. She put him on a treadmill, showed him his feet were over-pronating, explained injury risk, and offered to check his shoes. He spent $170. No pitching, no convincing — just questions that illuminated a real problem.

- **"Poke the bear" questions:** Ask a neutral question where either answer opens a conversation. Example: *"You've been in RevOps for 15 years — you've probably looked into automating commission calculations."* Not: "Have you considered automation?" — that implies they might not have thought of it, which insults a 15-year veteran. Either they say "yeah we looked at it but our rules were too complex" (great, now you know the real objection) or "actually no" (now you can educate). Both answers are useful.

- **Elicitation (FBI technique, popularised by Chris Voss):** Make a guess about their situation so the prospect corrects you and reveals information. *"I'm guessing your commission rules were too complex for automation."* They'll correct the guess and give you real data. This is how you get information without interrogating.

- **"I'm not sure if this is for you, but…"** — a specific phrase Josh recommends as a lead-in to any pitch. It removes the assumption of fit and lowers resistance before you've even started.

- **Sorting, not selling:** Josh reframes cold calling entirely. You are not trying to convince people — you are sorting them. A call where someone says "not interested" is not a negative result; it is a sort. He explicitly recommends removing the word "negative" from call dispositions and replacing it with "sorted out."

- Volume without signals is a low-probability activity. Josh cites data: from any raw list, only 2–6% of contacts are both reachable and ready to buy right now. This is why detachment and sorting are the only sustainable approaches — not because the channel doesn't work, but because the math of markets means most people will not buy from you right now.

- **Signal-based timing dramatically improves hit rate.** When outreach is tied to a trigger event (e.g., open enrollment for HR software, legislation requiring a product in schools, a specific conference happening this weekend), the percentage of people who are ready now goes up significantly. Spot Hero parking example: emailing people who all have tickets to the same concert, about parking near the venue, produces far higher relevance than a cold blast.

- **Staying top of mind without selling:** Josh's real estate agent example — the agent called, got a "no, love my home," and said *"Sounds like that's your forever home."* Josh corrected him ("definitely not forever") and they talked for a while. The agent ended by offering a comps newsletter. Josh subscribed. When he's ready to sell, he's calling that agent. The lesson: capture information from every conversation and find legitimate ways to stay useful over time without selling.

- On responding to objections: getting "no" doesn't mean stop. It means read the room. Ryan's example: prospect says "can you shoot me an email?" — he pauses and says *"That's not a problem. Would it be okay if I ask you a couple of questions just to see if that email would make sense?"* The prospect said yes. The point is not to overcome every objection, but to ask a question that opens a little more conversation — and be genuinely okay if the answer is still no.

### Key Insights

- Detachment is the foundation of Josh's entire outbound system — it changes the energy on a call more than any technique.
- The goal on every call is not a meeting. It is: "Is there a problem here?" Ask neutral questions, illuminate potential gaps, and accept whatever answer comes.
- "Poke the bear" + elicitation are Josh's two core question techniques — both designed to get information without triggering defensiveness or implying the prospect is unsophisticated.
- Sorting is a reframe that makes cold calling sustainable — every call has value whether it produces a meeting or not.
- Signal-based outreach changes the math; volume-only outreach without signals is fighting the base rates of 2–6% readiness.

## Video 2

**Title:** The Best Non-Salesy Cold Call Opener (Ft. Josh Braun)

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OJ2VUIlpJfQ

**Date:** May 10, 2026

### Transcript Summary

- Most cold callers start with openers that immediately trigger defensiveness — the prospect's nervous system goes on alert, they're looking for an exit, and the call is effectively over before it begins.

- Josh's solution starts **before the words** — with intent. If your intent is to book a meeting with everyone, it seeps into your behaviour and tone, and prospects feel the push. Detaching from the outcome is what makes the opener land correctly.

- **His go-to opener (verbatim):**
  *"Hey Chris, my name is Josh. We've never spoken, and I know you weren't expecting my call, but I was hoping you could help me out for a moment."*

- **Why each phrase works:**
  - *"We've never spoken"* — removes the "do I know this person?" anxiety immediately. You answer their first unspoken question before they ask it.
  - *"I know you weren't expecting my call"* — an empathy phrase that acknowledges the interruption and calms the prospect's nervous system.
  - *"I was hoping you could help me out"* — triggers a reciprocity instinct. Most people want to be helpful when asked directly and non-aggressively.

- With the right intent, **70–80% of the time** the prospect responds with "What is this about? How can I help you?" — and now you're in a conversation, not a pitch.

- There is no single correct opener. Josh uses and teaches 8–9 different openers. The best one is the one that feels authentic to you and flows naturally — a forced opener sounds worse than an imperfect one delivered with genuine calm.

### Key Insights

- The opener is the mechanical output of the right intent — it doesn't work if the intent is wrong.
- Each phrase in Josh's opener does specific psychological work: remove identity confusion, acknowledge the interruption, trigger helpfulness.
- 70–80% positive response rate is achievable with this opener when delivered with genuine detachment.
- There is no universal best opener — finding one that fits your natural voice matters more than using Josh's exact words.

## Why These Two?

Video 1 explains the philosophy that makes everything else work: detach from the outcome, sort rather than sell, illuminate problems rather than pitch, and use neutral questions that give the prospect full autonomy. Video 2 shows the tactical execution of that philosophy in the first 10 seconds of a call — an opener built on empathy, honesty, and reciprocity rather than hooks or pressure. Together they give a complete picture of Josh Braun's approach: mindset first, mechanics second.
